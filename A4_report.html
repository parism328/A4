Assignment 4: Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Paris Myers

Proposition: The NYPD's disciplinary system fails to hold officers accountable — officers with substantiated complaints face few meaningful consequences.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Rating: 2. The red color palette is intended to invite emotional polarization and anger without altering any data. The chart color and chart type do not conceal or change any data values.
  • The headline frames the statistic negatively — "89% of officers walk free" rather than "11% of cases result in charges" — both are the same statistic, said differently to back up the argument framing.
  • The intentional simplicity of the chart implies a perceived level of "objectivity," suggesting no fancy visuals or explanations are needed because the data is self-evidently damning. Red is also used to invite political polarization of the issue.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Rating: −1.5. While the data and stats are true, there are hidden assumptions in how data is selected ahead of time that result in a particular narrative — by focusing only on substantiated cases, the visualization implies 100% accountability while obscuring the vast majority of complaints that were never confirmed.
  • The visualization is intentionally more visually active — the use of large bold text and confident percentages are meant to almost overwhelm the senses, so viewers take the information claim as a given and focus on the emotional message rather than interrogating the data selection.
  • Blue tones are used to imply calmness and correctness, and to instigate politicization in two counter ways: for those sympathetic to police, navy blue signals solidarity ("blue lives matter"); for others, the modernist design signals objectivity and progressive values.

Final Reflection

I was quite surprised as to how weirdly fun it is to make deceptive visualizations and inflammatory headlines. I now feel like I understand why people make these wild arguments through persuasive charts — it's fun to get to explore the emotional power of language. For me, ethical analysis means not faking any numbers. There is freedom in how you may display the information, as it is yours to freely interpret, but under no circumstances should you delete or actually alter the data itself. I think if you actually delete or change the data itself that is unacceptable and pure falsification. I defined a persuasive choice as being 100% true in terms of what data is included (i.e. no data falsification), but framing the visualization (e.g. through language and visual perception) in a way that calls more or less attention to certain aspects of the visualization.

I was also surprised at how fast I became passionate about making these visualizations. Not from a moral point whatsoever — I don't personally or politically agree with either visualization approach. However, it was extremely fun getting behind an argument and thinking of the best way to "encode" this message subtly in the visualization. In general, I tend to write in a more implicitly persuasive way rather than explicitly persuasive, so at first I struggled with writing figure titles here. After googling examples in the real world, my brain almost immediately flipped into this old-school newspaper big-headline mode: "LOOK HERE! BIG NEWS!" You can see this difference between my first visualization and second — as I further flushed out more ethical "constraints" and "liberties" for myself I could then "play" with the visualization more.

Although I personally cannot stand language that emphasizes polarization or inflammation of arguments, these visualizations were incredibly fun and informative. It really helps you understand why these kinds of representations are everywhere: these kinds of graphs are not only addictive to consume, but also addictive to make!
